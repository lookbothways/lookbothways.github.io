When to go and how to get there

When should I go?

Quite frankly, any time of the year is great. It is a city break you’re after, isn’t it? With the occasional stop in a pub, café, restaurant…? In that case you don’t need to worry too much about monsoons, erupting volcanoes or any other such problems. Just get yourself ready for the continental weather.

From experience and talking to people who have been to Prague, we’ve learned that “I went in February and it was So Cold!!” Yes, if you are used to the mild-ish UK weather, you may think it’s cold. We, however look at it this way – there is no such thing as bad weather, only the wrong clothes. So dress well, check with us if unsure what to put in your suitcase and you’ll be just fine. We promise.

Every season has something great to offer. The spring is gorgeous, summer nice and hot, autumn spectacular and the winter crisp. Trust us, the Mulled wine from the Christmas markets on the Old Town Square will keep you warm. Just as much as the nice chilled pint of lager guarantees to cool you down in the heat of the continental summer.
You see, over there, we thought of everything.

So when should you go? Any time you fancy.

How do I get there?

The easiest way to get to Prague is by air. From experience we can recommend a couple of airlines that you would have heard of before now. Those are:

  • Easy Jet because they are usually the cheapest.
  • BA who offer quite a bit more comfort for just a little bit extra.
  • CSA (Czech Airlines) are good but you should check their price and compare with the two airlines above.
  • Another good place to check out is www.click4sky.com
  • And then you have your usual process of googling ‘cheap flights’ which will give you another half a million options.

In our days of sub zero income we used to travel a lot on coach but the companies who will take you directly from London to Prague are slowly disappearing and we are no longer able to recommend any one of the existing ones. But it’s still worth looking into a bit more if all you have is only a very little cash. We know of the Student Agency and Eurolines going to Prague.

Things to do before you go

The common sense ones

If you are traveling on your UK passport you should just be able to put it in your pocket and go. If however you do not have one of those, it’s best to check with the Czech Embassy in London. Conveniently enough, their website will also tell you the current temperature in Prague.

Your little plastic debit card will work just as well over there as it does here in the UK. So you don’t need to worry about getting too much cash before you leave the country. Excellent news, you’ll be saving on commission fees and you don’t need to guess how much you’re likely to spend. Just get the money out of the cash machine as and when you need it.
How much will you get for your UK pounds? There’s no point in us putting this info online – it has been almost 60Kc to £1 and it has also been about 30Kc to £1. You see, it tends to change quite a bit. To find out about the latest exchange rates from £ to Kc (Czech crown / 'Koruna') go to: www.xe.com

Now this is the best part – this holiday is one of those where you need nothing extra. No more jabs that you’d get at home, no malaria pills, no worries about drinking tap water when you’re over there... It’s all nice, easy and very civilized.

It may be worth checking out if your travel insurance is still valid and if you have your free European Health Card. Just in case. Or be very careful with combining too much mulled wine when it’s icy out there. You would not want to fall over and hurt yourself, would you? Having said that, the healthcare in Czech Republic is excellent so you’d have nothing to worry about if you did.

For more information check out the Practical information page on Czech tourism website.

Getting around Prague

Getting into town from the airport & getting around the town.

It could not be easier. First, before you even leave the airport, get yourself a 3 day or 5 day ticket, depending on how long you plan to stay. Then hop on bus No119, validate your ticket (in simple English - stick it in the little machine on the bus, it will print the start date and time for you on your ticket) and let it take you to the last bus stop ‘Dejvicka’, which is also the first stop on the tube. I can tell you with all honesty, the public transport could not be easier. There are only 3 tube lines - you cannot get lost! On top of that buses arrive on time, the tube is more spacious, and the trams are fun. Unless you’re arriving at the airport in the middle of the night, you should not need a taxi at all during your stay. This is another great money saving news for you.
Same as everything else, the cost of travel changes every so often and so we'd like to recomment that you check the Prague Public Transport website to get the most up to date info on cost and fares. Not to worry, even with prices going up, it will be much cheaper than moving around London. And another useful tip - do get a ticket, even if you don't see other people using theirs (they may have a season pass). You are very likely to be checked and it does sort of spoil the romance if you have to fork out for a fine.

Getting to the flat

This is also super-easy. The flat is in a fancy apartment block which is around the corner from the tube station Palmovka. From the flat to the tube platform it will take you about 3 minutes.
Palmovka station is 5 or 6 stops on the tube to the city centre (depending which part of the city centre you’re heading for).
What this means is that you will stay in an area that is not full of tourists but the moment you want to be in town, you need to give yourself no more than 1/2 hour and you’re there.
Once we confirm your dates and you get the keys to the flat, we will give you a detailed step by step (literaly!) instructions on how to find the flat. The only people who were not able to find it were... (well, I probably should not but I will tell you)... the couple who left these instructions in London!
Being out and how to enjoy the Real Czech Pub experience

Food and drink

This may happen to you too: every time we go, we have a long list of Czech delicatessen to work our way through. It’s not easy but it is possible. You just need to focus and stay away from the touristy places that will try to re-create your usual English diet.
You have traveled this far, you may as well go just as far with your eating adventures.
Have you ever had fried cheese in bread crumbs? Or what about dumplings with cabbage and pork? Trying out the traditional foods could fill your itinerary if you wanted to. We recommend that you don’t hold back and go for it. Ok, it’s a bit fattening but hey, when will you come back to enjoy another nice full plate? A week of reasonable eating back in the UK will put your weight back to normal. It’s been tried and tested many times (and I am still size 8ish)
Slight difficulty may arise if you are a vegetarian or even vegan. There a places that offer more than just a couple of meat-free dishes but if you stick mainly to the traditional, local Czech pubs and restaurants, you may not have so much to choose from. We apologies on behalf of all meat eating Czechs but this is the way it seems to be over there…

How to order your food a drink

When it comes to dining and wining (or drinking beer, which is just so much more likely) we recommend that you have your phrase book handy so that you know what it is you’re ordering & that you try a few little words, at least your ‘please’ and ‘thank you’. We can pretty much guarantee that the service will go up a notch or two. Throughout history the Czechs always had to speak more than just their own language. Recently those have been mainly German and Russian, and so they will appreciate when you, an English speaker make a little bit of an effort.
Go on, don’t by shy. It’s fun. You may even get so much into it that you’ll end up signing up for a Czech language course at the Czech centre in London.
Let me make it even easier for you - my dictionary and phrase book is in the kitchen. Borrow it & use it, ok.

A few bits about pub etiquette

We will tell you about the traditional places, ok. Not the fancy, set up for tourist ones. We don’t go to those – ever.
You can just walk in and take your seat. Always expect to be served. There is no need to queue at a bar and wait for your drink. It will come to you.
If you order a ‘pint’ it will be in fact half a liter that you get (you will have noticed by now that we don’t do pints, we operate in metric measurements). The beer will come with a nice big head which will slowly but surely settle right at the line which tells you where half liter comes to. Just give it a minute. You are not being short changed. Czech people don’t mess about when it comes to their beer. It’s a very serious matter, you see.
Now for the other thing - tipping. It is customary to tip the person when they present you with a bill. We don’t leave change on the table when we leave. Let me give you an example. The waitress comes over and tells you that your bill comes to 153Kc. To this you say ‘170’. She thanks you and the transaction is done. You round up the number and / or add a bit extra. That’s all. Nice and simple. Now you've qualified for the Real Czech Pub Experience. Well done.
Prague - what is it all about?

One of the most romantic cities in Europe, Prague is culturally diverse & vibrant; historical city that offers a lot to all visitors.

The origin of Prague

The origin of Prague goes back to the 7th century and the Slavic princess Libuše, a woman of great beauty and wisdom who possessed prophetic powers. Libuše and her husband, prince Přemysl, ruled peacefully over the Czech lands from the hill of Vyšehrad (BTW a place worth visiting). A legend says that one day Libuše had a vision. She stood on a cliff overlooking the river Vltava, pointed to a forested hill across the river, and proclaimed: "I see a great city whose glory will touch the stars." ("Vidím město veliké, jehož sláva hvězd se dotýkati bude."). She instructed her people to go and build a castle where a man was building the threshold (in Czech práh) of a house. "And because even the great noblemen must bow low before a threshold, you shall give it the name Praha". Her words were obeyed and some two hundred years later, the city of Prague became the seat of the Premyslid dynasty.
What an excellent story, don’t you think?

Prague today

Here is what the experts say: Prague, the Czech capital (please do not, under any circumstances call it Czechoslovakia and ideally, have a look at the map of Europe to see that calling it Eastern Europe is not the smartest thing either), is among the fastest-developing and most progressive regions in all of Europe. Over the past few years, the city has transformed itself from what some seen as a ‘dreary East Bloc capital’ to a modern European metropolis.
The 1.3 million-strong capital is among the most visited cities in central Europe and, understandably, the most popular Czech destination. Prague is a living textbook of different architectural styles, where Romanesque, Gothic, Renaissance, and Baroque structures stand side by side with examples of Art Nouveau, Cubist, and functionalist architecture.
Prague enjoys a vibrant cultural and social life. Dozens of galleries and museums attract visitors to their permanent collections and temporary exhibitions, displaying artistic and craft treasures of all periods and places of origin. Even if this is not exactly your cup of tea, it’s worth going to at least one exhibition.
And what do we say about Prague today? It's brilliant, beautiful and it has a soul. It's a place you'll love forever.

Dangers and annoyances

Are you arriving to Prague from London? Well then you will be surprised how peaceful and quiet the city is (unless, of course you bump into a hen or stag do which flew in on the same plane). You can slow down a bit but it doesn’t mean that you should forget to pay attention to what’s going on around you.
The most likely thing is that someone will try to pickpocket you. This is a city, and so sadly, it is somewhat to be expected. Just keep an eye on your bag and you’ll be fine. We have not heard of any other kinds of incidents and it’s highly unlikely that anything worse will happen to you. Feeling relieved? Good, so are we.

Since I mentioned 'peaceful and quiet' - please try to keep those two words in mind when you're arriving at the flat late at night, having spent the day out, enjoying yourself. You're on holiday, we know. But the chance is that most of the people around you aren't and are in fact getting up very early to go to work.
What is there to do and see?

You can start with the most obvious activity - city walks and sightseeing. Especially if this is your first time in the Czech Republic, this is where you need to start.
Enjoy the Old Town Square, be careful not to hurt your neck as you check out the astronomical clock, stop for a concert, look at the shops on Wenceslas Square, walk to the river, cross over Charles Bridge, walk up the hill through Mala Strana to the Castle and see the cathedral 'Sv Vita' there. Most definitely have your camera ready to take some snaps of the red rooftops. Take the cable car to the top to Petrin Hill and walk up the little 'Eiffel' Tower and wonder around the Mirror Maze, and then down the hill, enjoying the greenery around you. Just go wherever you see something pretty, something worth taking a picture of. If you do nothing else but follow this one piece of advice, you’re guaranteed to keep walking for a good couple of days at least.

Please note that it's worth your time to chek out opening times online, before you actually go to see something. There is small chance that not everything will be open just when you need it. We have been told that the mini Eiffel Tower and the Mirror Maze on top of Petrin Hill have restricted opening hours in winter. Try the Prague Inforamtion Service site for more up to date info.

Don't get hungry
Another one of the reasons why you're travelling to Prague is to enjoy the food and drink, right? Have a pint in the Staropramen Brewery Pub or just any Czech lager anywhere you fancy. Do you prefer Bernard, Gambrinus, Plzen, 'Star', Svijany or a pint of the original Budvar? Whichever one it is, you are probably about 5minutes away from it.
Czech traditional food is another thing you will remember for a long time - did you ever realise how much you can eat in one holiday? No, you didn't but you're about to. Wander around and try whatver you fancy. 'Goulash', dumplings, fried cheese... a lot of yummy (often fried) food. Enjoy!

High brow culture
Whilst you’re at it, walking your feet off, you can stop for a concert or perhaps even an opera at the National Theatre. We love to do that. The price you pay for your tickets is half of what you’d pay for the same performance in London. The theater has a great history & the performances and simply wonderful. If your concern is not understanding, than you need to worry no more – each opera will have subtitles in both English and German. Easy.
After the show you might want to pop into ‘Kmotra’, the famous pizzeria just around the corner from the National Theatre. There you are likely to sit at the table next to the one with all the stars. We got to see Mr Nutcracker there much better than we got to see him from our fancy seats at the ballet.

Museums
Plenty of those around so just have your pick. I personally very much like the Technical museum. Old trains, planes, automobiles… and then all those technical things I simply don’t understand that well. Fascinating place to visit.
Our guests also recommended the main Museum on the top of Wenceslas Square ('Vaclavske namesti') and with that issues a warning - you will need a full day to see the whole place. Chose just one or two subjects and get out after a couple of hours. There is so much more to see.
Czech republic has a rich history and so whichever aspect of it interests you, you can find out more by visiting one of the many museum. Just do your research before you go to be sure that it’s open on the day you want to go. Most places used to close on Mondays but that might have changed.

River cruise
Here comes the big confession – we have never been on a river cruise in Prague but some of our English friends do recommend it as a romantic thing to do.

Movie buff?
You will not miss on any of the movies that will be on in the UK whilst you’re away. You can see the film that you’ve been waiting for and what’s even better, for about half the price again. Isn’t it just great? What’s even better is that it's pretty likely that you might be able to ‘czech out’ some of the locations. Mission Impossible, Bourne Identity, The League of Extraordinary Gentlemen, Hellboy, xXx, Blade 1&2, Amadeus, The Illusionist, Kolya, Oliver Twist, Les Miserables, Faust and many others.
If you are up for a day trip you can see where one of the latest Bond movies was filmed. A couple of hours scenic train journey and you are there - Karlovy Vary, a gorgeous palce.

Prague ZOO – something special
I guess that you are most likely not going for this city break with a little kid but whether you are or you are not, the Prague ZOO is one of the places we visit pretty much every time we are there long enough to spare a day. It is simply fabulous. Oh go on - we know you always wanted to visit a bat cave...

Sights outside of the city
There are some wonderful spas within no more than a couple of hours coach or train ride from Prague. If you are staying long enough, those are well worth visiting.
Another good idea for a day trip out of Prague is a visit to one of the many castles or chateaus. The most famous ones are on offer with any tour operator in the city. However, since we use public transport, we recommend that you do the same. Save a bit of cash, stay as long as you want to and leave behind the other tourists. You didn’t come here to hang out with other foreigners, did you?

Sports fan?

Football
Well, there are a few options here. You can go and see one of the local football matches. We support Slavia. Our ‘enemies’ are Sparta and I guess the best match you can get is when those two teams play each other.

Running
If you are into running (and I know that quite a few of you, my friends, are), you can take part in the Prague International Marathon (usually May) or any other similar event. And if you’re not too sure about being able to make it but you still like to run, you can try something else – for example the 'Prague Relative 10k'. This run is particularly great if you like running and also enjoy good beer, all at the same time. The way it works is quite funny: you are given the opportunity to have one large beer (0.5l) four times during the race. For every beer you have, your final time gets improved. You see, being all skinny and healthy is all good, but here it’s a different thing all together. This race welcomes beer drinkers. Bizarre, right. And I hear it’s a lot of fun too.

Skiing
The whole of the Czech republic is surrounded by mountains. So just have your pick which way you fancy going skiing. We recommend hoping on the tube to 'Cerny Most' station and from there taking the coach which will (for a few quid / in about 2hrs) get you to a ski resort. There you can hire all the stuff you need and enjoy the snow.
Do you remember last time you saw proper snow in the UK? Well, there you go. Again, as you are earning in UK pounds, the cost of a few days skiing will be something you will tell you friends about - ‘cheap as chips mate!’

Rock climbing
Ok, if you are a proper hard-core climber and can’t leave the wall for a day or two but your girlfriend so wants to go for this city break, here is your good news. Two streets away from us is a climbing wall and just a tram ride away is the Boulder Bar.
And if you are both keen climbers, it gets even better. Do you remember what I said in the skiing chapter? Mountains, mountains everywhere. Proper mountains, not just peaks. You’ll love them. Start with a couple of days in Prague, then head out to the countryside and have fun.

Hiking, trekking and mountain biking
I am going to take you back to the mountains that I mentioned above. You can have a wonderful time there. We have been to places where we’ve met just a handful of people during an entire day of walking. Peace, quiet, greenery. Anything else you can ask for? Sure, a couple of days in Prague before you set of.
Any recommendations or challenges? Yes, try to get to the peak of Snezka, the tallest of all Czech mountains, 1602meters (now, try converting this into feet… and… now you’re impressed, right)

Camping
And if you are a proper adventurer (or a budget holiday maker with some sense for adventure), here is another suggestion – camping holiday in Czech. We do not have a seaside but the numerous lakes compensate for this perfectly. Woods behind you, gorgeous lake in front of you, clean air, the birds are singing… what else do you want? The weather is pretty much guaranteed (unlike the gamble you need to take in the UK every July).
In some places you can hire kayaks, boats; you can even have a go at windsurfing. Frankly, whatever you want, you can find it.

'Lofty Palmovka' is a new development in one of Prague's central districts, only a few metro stops from the city centre.

The building and its location

This three-storey former brick factory dates from the 19th century. The original structure has been preserved and is unique: its high ceilings, big windows, impressive arches and wooden beams go so well together, creating a genuinely unique environment.

The building is within 2 minutes walking distance from the nearest tube, Palmovka, which in turn is within a few stops on the tube from many historic sites and places of interest that you may like to visit while in Prague.

Even though the building is close to the local amenities, it is set back from the main road & our apartment faces a private courtyard, guaranteeing a peaceful night in.

The district

Palmovka has a riverside park with a beautiful chateau which is only 10 minutes walk. Here you can find a quiet space to enjoy, away from the vibrant city centre.

The area also offers a great range of pubs, bars and restaurants, much cheaper than those in the city centre. You can find the perfect breakfast in the local bakery & in the evenings try traditional Czech food or opt for pizza if you feel less adventurous. And of course, there's always the option to cook in the apartment as there are many shops and some larger supermarkets near the tube station.

The recently built 'O2 Arena' (previously known as Sazka Arena) is only 15 minutes walk and it is the location of many interesting events.
